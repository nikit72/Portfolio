IDEAS

Tasked with the goal to enhance recreation, we brainstormed different recreational activities that had needs we could tackle. As all art lovers, we narrowed it to enhancing art forms since we saw many potential areas for improvement in art. To insure that we would have a lot of concepts to work with, we decided to observe and interview people who engaged in three separate art forms: comedy, dance, and visual arts. While they do vary drastically in how they are performed, observing activities which such distinctions would allow us to explore many needs, as well as see their potential overlap.

FIND THOSE NEEDS

Art is such a diverse topic! And we wanted to make sure to get those diverse experiences. We began our exploration by observing and interviewing people in order to view art in their eyes. Having both observations and interviews allowed us to see what problems artists were aware of and what problems they might not notice while they create.

As the activities we targeted varied greatly, there were many interesting notes that came up. One of which that caught our attention across the three art forms was the need to map the creative process and keep track of ideas that spur when you least expect it.

STORYBOARDS

To get a better sense of how we could target this need, we began to storyboard what a potential user’s creative process might look like. This would give more insight on their behaviors and actions when a problem might occur.

One insight we found really changed how we wanted to target the user’s needs. Not only did we want our app to help organize the creative process, we wanted to insure that our user would stay inspired and motivated as they created as that had a big impact on their work.

PAPER PROTOTYPES

With our storyboards to guide us, the next step was creating paper prototypes of how the user’s interaction with our app might occur. The creative art process is very abstract and so how we framed our app would have to fit that unique experience while having a uniform design for multiple users. WIREFRAMES

While ideating many different possible pages our app could have, we came up with several main pages and functions. We then created wireframes of the main features of our app to guide us in our functional prototype.

USER TESTING

With our prototypes, we then began to do user testing to see if the functions we added were needed in the first place and if the task flows were intuitive. What we found was the appeal of the sprout page and that some functions were more intuitive than others. These notes helped us understand which functions we had to scaffold for the user and how to improve the user’s interactions with the app. Below are some examples of the changes we made.

One change we made was the navbar. We found that it was better to declutter the navbar by turning the "Plant Project" function into a popup rather than add it via the navbar. We also moved the function next to the home button so people would not mistakenly logout.

Another change was scaffolding the "Sprout leaf" function for new users. We found that most users tended to click on the instuction box rather than the button on the navbar. With this information, we decided to turn the description into the button itself. This also encourages people to continue to grow the treeline as the function as now a part of it like a growing tree.

COMPARATIVE EVALUATION

To ensure that our redesign of the "Sprout Leaf" function was indeed needed, we then tested the 2 versions to see which would be more intuitive for the users. With A/B testing, we found that the redesign decreases the time it take for the new user to click on the function, which was our goal.

FINAL

With a lot of changes made throughout the process, we were able to create a prototype that is now up and running for testing. You can plant a project and sprout with us too!
